Abstract
Multivariate Time Series Forecasting (MTSF) is a fundamental problem with wide applications in energy
systems, climate modelling, financial analysis, and traffic monitoring. The SimpleTM framework [1]
proposes a lightweight yet competitive architecture that employs Stationary Wavelet Transform (SWT)
tokenization and a Clifford-algebra-inspired geometric attention mechanism. However, its fixed single-
strategy tokenisation may not be optimal across datasets exhibiting heterogeneous signal characteristics.
This projectΓÇöSimpleTM++ΓÇösystematically investigates whether replacing or extending the original
SWT tokeniser with an FFT-based tokeniser, or combining multiple tokenisation strategies through
adaptive fusion, can improve forecasting robustness and accuracy. Work completed at mid-semester
includes: a thorough literature review, full understanding of the SimpleTM codebase, multi-dataset ex-
ploratory data analysis (EDA), implementation of both the SimpleTM SWT baseline and the SimpleTM FFT
variant, and experimental evaluation on two ETT benchmark datasets. On ETTm1 (seq = 96, pred = 96),
SimpleTM SWT achieves MSE = 0.3497 while SimpleTM FFT achieves MSE = 0.3630ΓÇöan unexpected gap
given the strong frequency-domain structure observed in EDA. The report analyses this discrepancy in
depth and motivates why a hybrid tokenisation approach remains the correct long-term direction. The
planned SimpleTM++ hybrid model fuses SWT, FFT, and local convolutional branches through learnable
gating, and is expected to overcome the limitations of each individual tokeniser.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Time-series forecasting is the task of predicting future values of a signal using historical observations. In the
multivariate setting, N variables evolve jointly over time and the forecaster must exploit both temporal
dependencies within each channel and inter-channel correlations. Accurate multivariate forecasting
underpins a broad set of high-impact applications:
ΓÇó Electricity demand and load estimation [5];
ΓÇó Weather and climate prediction;
ΓÇó Traffic flow and road-occupancy monitoring;
ΓÇó Industrial sensor analysis and anomaly detection;
ΓÇó Financial price and volatility modelling.
Deep learning, particularly Transformer-based models, has achieved state-of-the-art performance on
standard MTSF benchmarks. However, large Transformer modelsΓÇöincluding LLM-adapter variants and
architectures such as iTransformer and PatchTSTΓÇörequire substantial computational resources and
extensive domain-specific fine-tuning. This limits their applicability in resource-constrained or real-time
settings.
1.2
Motivation
SimpleTM [1] was proposed as a lightweight, specialised alternative. By combining SWT-based tokenisation
with geometric attention (combining dot-product and exterior product scores), even a one- or two-layer
model achieves results competitive with much larger architectures on standard benchmarks. This raises a
deeper question: is the SWT tokenisation the optimal choice for all datasets, or can other signal-processing
representations provide complementary or superior inductive biases?
EDA on the ETT datasets reveals that real-world time series exhibit qualitatively different structures:
ΓÇó Strong periodic components visible in FFT spectra (motivating FFT tokenisation);
ΓÇó Multi-scale non-stationary dynamics captured by wavelet decomposition (motivating SWT
tokenisation);
ΓÇó Short-range local motifs and abrupt spikes (motivating convolutional tokenisation).
No single tokeniser is universally optimal; this motivates the SimpleTM++ hybrid framework proposed
in this project.

Chapter 2
Literature Review
2.1
Multivariate Time Series Forecasting
Multivariate time series forecasting has been addressed through a wide range of modelling paradigms.
Classical statistical methods such as ARIMA and VAR provide interpretable models but fail to capture
complex nonlinear dependencies. Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and their gated variants (LSTM,
GRU) model sequential dependencies but suffer from gradient issues over long horizons.
Transformer-based models [5] have transformed sequence modelling broadly. In the time-series domain,
Informer [6] introduced sparse attention for long-sequence forecasting. Autoformer [7] incorporated
a decomposition block (trend + seasonality) into the Transformer structure. FEDformer leveraged
frequency-enhanced attention. PatchTST [8] introduced patch-based tokenisation to better capture
local temporal semantics. TimeMixer combined multi-scale mixing of seasonal and trend information.
iTransformer inverted the attention axis to operate over variate tokens rather than time steps.
A common challenge across all these models is that they are relatively large and computationally
expensive, requiring significant memory and often dataset-specific fine-tuning.
2.2
SimpleTM
SimpleTM [1] was accepted at ICLR 2025 and proposes a radically lightweight alternative. Its key
innovations are:
1. Inverted embedding: treats each variate as a token, mapping x ΓêêRB├ùL├ùN to z ΓêêRB├ùN├ùd via
a linear projection over time.
2. SWT tokenisation: applies the Stationary Wavelet Transform (SWT) to decompose each token
into m + 1 frequency-scale sub-bands, producing multi-scale representations without downsampling.
3. Geometric attention: replaces standard dot-product attention with a Clifford-algebra-inspired
score combining a dot product and a wedge product (exterior product magnitude):

CHAPTER 2. LITERATURE REVIEW
8
The OpenReview peer assessments [2] highlight several strengths: simplicity, low memory footprint,
competitive performance across 8 datasets, and well-motivated architecture. Key weaknesses raised by
reviewers include: lack of characterisation of alternative tokenisation schemes (Reviewer L7y3 specifically
calls out the absence of DFT-based ablation), minimal ablation over the SWT contribution itself
(Reviewers Lbi6 and avrv), and no statistical significance tests for performance differences. This BTP
directly addresses the first two gaps.
2.3
Wavelet-Based Methods
Wavelet transforms have been applied extensively in signal processing for time-frequency analysis.
The SWT (also called the undecimated wavelet transform) preserves the length of the signal at each
decomposition level by avoiding downsampling, providing time-shift invariance. This makes it attractive for
forecasting where temporal alignment is critical. Learnable wavelet filters further allow the decomposition
to adapt to data-driven frequency structures.
2.4
Frequency-Domain Methods
Fourier analysis decomposes a signal into a sum of sinusoidal components. The FFT provides a global
frequency representation of the entire sequence. FEDformer [9] showed that operating in the frequency
domain can improve forecasting by directly modelling periodic structures. However, the FFT assumes
global stationarityΓÇöthe frequency content is assumed to be constant across the entire sequenceΓÇöwhich is
violated by many real-world time series exhibiting time-varying dynamics.
2.5
Hybrid and Multi-Scale Representations
Several recent works have explored hybrid representations combining time-domain and frequency-domain
information. TimeMixer uses multi-resolution temporal mixing. Multi-scale patch approaches combine
local and global representations. The general principle is that complementary representations, combined
adaptively, can overcome the limitations of any single view of the signal.
2.6
Research Gap
The original SimpleTM uses a fixed SWT tokeniser regardless of the dataset. Reviewer L7y3 at ICLR
2025 explicitly asked for ΓÇ£characterisation of other methods such as discrete Fourier transforms in the
context of the proposed tokenisation schemeΓÇ¥ [2]. This BTP addresses this gap directly by implementing
a controlled FFT tokeniser ablation and studying the empirical behaviour of frequency-domain and
wavelet-domain tokenisation on two standard benchmark datasets.

Chapter 4
Methodology
4.1
Overview
The project follows a structured empirical methodology:
1. Codebase Study: Thorough analysis of the SimpleTM official repository.
2. EDA: Multi-dimensional signal analysis across benchmark datasets.
3. Controlled Ablation: Vary only the tokenisation module; keep all other components (attention,
embedding, normalisation, optimiser, hyperparameters) identical.
4. Metric Evaluation: Report MSE, MAE, RMSE, MAPE, and MSPE on held-out test sets.
5. Analysis: Explain observed results via signal-processing arguments and EDA findings.
6. Hybrid Design: Propose and implement the SimpleTM++ architecture informed by the ablation
analysis.
4.2
Dataset Selection
The ETTh1 and ETTm1 datasets were selected because they are standard benchmarks reported in the
SimpleTM paper, they differ in temporal granularity (hourly vs. 15-minute), and they exhibit different
signal characteristics as revealed by EDA. All seven variates (oil temperature, four load features, two
transformer metrics) are used in the multivariate forecasting setting (--features M).
4.3
Exploratory Data Analysis
EDA was performed prior to model modification using the SimpleTM EDA Full.ipynb notebook. The
analysis covered:
ΓÇó Summary statistics: Mean, standard deviation, value range, missing values.
ΓÇó Raw time-series plots with rolling mean and rolling standard deviation.
ΓÇó Stationarity analysis: Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test per variate, producing stationarity
percentages per dataset.
ΓÇó FFT spectrum analysis: Power spectral density plots to quantify dominant periodicities and
spectral concentration.
ΓÇó Wavelet decomposition: Multi-level SWT to visualise multi-scale structure and energy distribu-
tion across scales.
ΓÇó Cross-channel correlation: Heatmaps of Pearson correlation across the 7 ETT variates.

System Architecture
5.1
Original SimpleTM Pipeline
Figure 5.1: Original SimpleTM architecture. SWT tokenisation decomposes each variate token into m + 1
sub-bands; geometric attention operates over this multi-scale representation.
The original SimpleTM pipeline operates as follows:
1. Instance Normalisation (RevIN): Subtracts the channel mean and divides by standard deviation;
statistics are stored for de-normalisation.
2. Inverted Embedding: Projects (B, L, N) ΓåÆ(B, N, d) by transposing and applying a linear
projection over the time axis.
3. SWT Decomposition: (B, N, d) ΓåÆ(B, N, m+1, d) via multi-level SWT with Daubechies-1 (Haar)
filters.
4. Q/K/V Projection: Three separate linear projections with dropout.
5. Geometric Attention: Computes scores as (1 ΓêÆ╬▒) dot + ╬▒ wedge.
6. SWT Reconstruction (ISWT): (B, N, m + 1, d) ΓåÆ(B, N, d).
7. Feed-Forward Network: Two 1D convolutional layers with GELU activation.
8. Output Projection: (B, N, d) ΓåÆ(B, H, N).
9. De-normalisation: Reverses the RevIN step.

Chapter 8
Discussion
8.1
Why the FFT Model Underperforms on ETTm1
The EDA suggested that ETTm1 has strong, concentrated spectral peaksΓÇöa property that intuitively
favours FFT tokenisation. However, the experimental results show a 3.8% MSE degradation for the FFT
model. This apparent contradiction is explained by the following signal-processing arguments:
8.1.1
Stationarity Assumption Violation
The FFT assumes global stationarity: frequency content is assumed to be constant over the entire sequence
window. ETTm1, despite having strong spectral peaks in aggregate, exhibits time-varying frequency
structureΓÇöthe phase and amplitude of periodic components change over time (e.g., load patterns shift
between weekdays and weekends, or across seasons). The SWT preserves temporal localisation of
frequency events by operating on a sliding-window basis; the FFT discards this localisation.
8.1.2
Phase Information Loss
The FFT band decomposition implemented in this project retains magnitude structure but effectively loses
fine-grained phase information during the band-splitting process. Forecasting performance depends
critically on correctly predicting the phase of recurring events (e.g., the precise timing of the next load
peak). SWT retains phase by operating in the time domain at each decomposition level.
8.1.3
Over-Smoothing from Spectral Averaging
Dividing the spectrum into m + 1 = 4 hard bands and reconstructing with irfft effectively applies a
bank of ideal bandpass filters. Ideal bandpass filters in the frequency domain correspond to sinc-function
convolutions in the time domain, introducing Gibbs ringing artefacts. SWT uses compactly-supported
wavelet filters (db1/Haar) that avoid this issue.
8.1.4
Short-Sequence Context
With seq len=96, the time series window contains only 96 samples. The frequency resolution of the FFT
is Γêåf = 1/96 Γëê0.01 cycles per step. At this resolution, many closely-spaced spectral peaks of ETTm1
are aliased into the same frequency band, reducing the discriminative power of the FFT decomposition.
The SWT uses multi-scale convolutions that are not resolution-limited in the same way.

8.1.5
Implementation Axis Consideration
The current FFT tokeniser applies the transform along the d model embedding dimension after the
inverted linear projection. Ideally, FFT should be applied along the temporal axis (dimension L) before
or during embedding to directly exploit the temporal periodicity identified in EDA. This is a known
implementation gap identified during the project and is listed as a future improvement.
8.2
Why We Should Still Pursue the Hybrid Model
Despite the FFT modelΓÇÖs underperformance, the hybrid approach remains strongly motivated for the
following reasons:
Complementary Information.
The EDA shows that ETTm1 simultaneously contains all three
types of structure: periodic components (captured by FFT), multi-scale non-stationary dynamics
(captured by SWT), and local abrupt changes (captured by local convolutions). No single tokeniser
captures all three. A hybrid model with learnable gating weights can, in principle, combine these
complementary views.
FFT is Not UselessΓÇöIt Captures Different Structure.
The FFT model achieves MSPE =
350.89 on ETTm1 versus 376.18 for SWTΓÇöa 6.7% improvement in MSPE despite worse MSE. This
suggests that the FFT model better captures the relative magnitude patterns (periodic peaks), while the
SWT model better captures absolute deviations. A hybrid can potentially achieve lower error on both
metrics.
Limitations of Current FFT Implementation are Addressable.
The three main issuesΓÇöstationarity
violation, short context length, and the wrong transform axisΓÇöare all addressable through engineering:
ΓÇó Apply FFT along the temporal axis (pre-embedding);
ΓÇó Use longer context windows (L = 336 or L = 720) to improve frequency resolution;
ΓÇó Use short-time Fourier transform (STFT) or learnable filterbanks to address non-stationarity within
the FFT branch.
When these fixes are applied and the FFT branch is used as one component of a fusion model (rather
than a standalone tokeniser), its contribution can be positive even on ETTm1.
Reviewer Evidence.
ICLR 2025 Reviewer L7y3 [2] explicitly asked: ΓÇ£it would be beneficial to
characterise other methods [such as DFT] in the context of the proposed tokenisation scheme.ΓÇ¥ This
BTP provides exactly that characterisation and establishes the experimental baseline for hybrid fusion.
EDA-Predicted Hybrid Superiority.
Table 6.2 shows that for datasets with co-existing signal types
(mixed stationarity + periodicity + local volatility), a hybrid tokeniser is theoretically optimal. The
theoretical prediction of the hybrid is that it should achieve lower MSE than either SWT or FFT alone on
ETTm1, because it can route the periodic component through the FFT branch and the transient/trend
component through the SWT branch.

Limitations
9.1
Current Implementation Limitations
L1. FFT axis mismatch: The current FFTEmbedding applies the transform along the d model dimension
(after the inverted linear embedding) rather than the temporal axis. This means the FFT is not
directly exploiting the time-domain periodicity identified in EDA. A corrected implementation would
apply rfft along dimension L before embedding.
L2. Short context window: All experiments use seq len=96. At 15-minute granularity (ETTm1),
this corresponds to only 24 hours of history. The FFT frequency resolution at this window size is
Γêåf = 1/96, limiting the ability to resolve closely-spaced spectral peaks. The SimpleTM paperΓÇÖs area
chair recommends evaluating longer context lengths for fairer comparison.
L3. Single prediction horizon: Only pred len=96 was evaluated. The behaviour of FFT vs. SWT
tokenisation may differ at longer horizons (192, 336, 720), particularly because longer horizons
require capturing low-frequency trends that SWT handles better.
L4. Limited dataset coverage: Experiments are currently limited to ETTh1 and ETTm1. The relative
advantage of different tokenisers may vary significantly on other datasets (Weather, ECL, Traffic)
that have different dimensionalities and signal characteristics.
L5. No statistical significance testing: Each model was trained with a single fixed random seed
(2025). ICLR 2025 Reviewer avrv [2] specifically noted the absence of error bars and multiple-seed
evaluation. With a single seed, the observed MSE differences (1ΓÇô4%) cannot be declared statistically
significant.
L6. Hard frequency band partition: The current FFT tokeniser uses hard (non-overlapping, rectan-
gular) frequency band masks. Hard masks cause Gibbs artefacts in the reconstructed time-domain
signals. Overlapping soft Gaussian masks or learnable filterbanks would be more appropriate.
L7. Hybrid model not yet implemented: The core contribution of the project (the SimpleTM++
hybrid architecture) remains in the design phase. The mid-semester deliverable establishes the
baselines and analysis; the hybrid implementation is the primary remaining task.
L8. No ablation over ╬▒ and m: The geometric attention weight ╬▒ and decomposition depth m were
fixed at 1.0 and 3 respectively. An ablation over these hyperparameters for each tokeniser could
reveal configuration-dependent interactions.

Chapter 11
Conclusion
This mid-semester report presents the progress of the SimpleTM++ B.Tech Project, which investigates
adaptive tokenisation strategies for lightweight multivariate time series forecasting. Building on the
SimpleTM framework [1] accepted at ICLR 2025, the project implements a controlled ablation between
SWT and FFT tokenisation strategies, while keeping the geometric attention mechanism unchanged.
The key experimental finding is that SimpleTM FFT underperforms SimpleTM SWT on both ETTh1
and ETTm1, with the gap being more pronounced on ETTm1 (3.8% MSE). This is counterintuitive
given the strong periodic structure observed in EDA, and is attributed to the FFTΓÇÖs global stationarity
assumption, phase information loss, and the short-context frequency resolution limitation.
Despite this result, the hybrid model remains strongly motivated. The EDA confirms that ETTm1
exhibits co-existing signal types that no single tokeniser can fully capture, the FFT model exhibits
complementary behaviour to SWT on the MSPE metric, and the FFT implementation gaps are addressable
through axis correction and longer context evaluation.
The remaining project work focuses on implementing the full SimpleTM++ hybrid architecture, expand-
ing the experimental evaluation to multiple datasets and horizons, and conducting multi-seed statistical
analysis.
